1. Reciprocal Function – Pirate Ship

Sunway Lagoon, Malaysia

Parent Function

\( f(x) = \dfrac{1}{x} \)

Below is an image of the ideal ride that best matches the parent function, which helps visualize why we chose this ride.

When we observe the ship near the bottom of its swing, the curved shape evokes the way that reciprocal functions’ graph bends as they approach asymptotes.

Transformed Functions

First Function

\( g(x) = -45.8\left(\dfrac{1}{x-10.9}\right) - 12,\quad 0.5 \le x \le 7.1 \)

  • Reflection across the x-axis
  • Vertical stretch by a factor of 45.8
  • Horizontal translation 10.9 units to the right
  • Vertical translation 12 units downwards

Second Function

\( h(x) = -3.9\left(\dfrac{1}{1.8(x-8)}\right) - 2.3,\quad 7.1 \le x \le 7.65 \)

  • Reflection across the x-axis
  • Vertical stretch by a factor of \(3.9/1.8\)
  • Horizontal translation 8 units to the right
  • Vertical translation 2.3 units downwards

Explanation

First, to make the function show a growing trend, we applied a reflection, which could be done over either the x or y axis. In our case, we chose the x-axis. As \(x\) approaches 0, the function begins to change rapidly because \(x\) is in the denominator. So, we decided to apply a vertical stretch. We found that it best fits the bottom of the pirate ship when the value of \(a\) is between 40 and 50, ultimately settling on 45.8. Finally, we adjusted the asymptote to the pirate ship's position by adding a horizontal translation of 10.9 units to the right and a vertical translation of 12 units downwards.

To capture the remaining portion, we add the second function. Similar to the first function, this one also needs to be reflected first. Since the remaining part of the pirate ship is quite steep, no significant changes were needed. We began with a vertical stretch, then aligned the curve with the pirate ship by applying a horizontal translation of 8 units to the right and a vertical translation of 2.3 units downwards. At this point, the top of the curve is positioned very close to the top of the pirate ship, so we applied a horizontal compression by a factor of 1.8 and adjusted the vertical stretch from 4 to 3.9 to make the function’s graph fit the entire ride.

Domain and Range

For the first function, it fits from the leftmost point of the pirate ship near \((0.5,-7.6)\) to \((7.1,0.05)\), thus:

  • \(g(x)\) Domain: \([0.5, 7.1]\)
  • \(g(x)\) Range: \([-7.6, 0.05]\) (rounded)

The second function continues from \(x = 7.1\) to \(x = 7.65\), reaching approximately \((7.65,3.89)\). Although the horizontal span is short, the curve is steep, producing a large vertical change; hence:

  • \(h(x)\) Domain: \([7.1, 7.65]\)
  • \(h(x)\) Range: \([0.11, 3.89]\) (rounded)

2. Reciprocal of x Squared Function

Halloween Entrance – Sunway Lagoon

The second type of function is the reciprocal of \(x^2\) function. With Halloween approaching, the amusement park is filled with various seasonal decorations. This ride adopts a horror theme—decorated with a skull and two pirate cutlasses. You will see it as soon as you walk into the park. Come and try! Good luck!

Parent Function

\( f(x) = \dfrac{1}{x^2} \)

Below is an image of the ideal ride that best matches the parent function, which helps visualize why we chose this ride.

Below the two pirate cutlasses, there are two excellent arcs that match the reciprocal of \(x^2\) function perfectly. The pirate cutlass on the left is like a function growing from the third to first quadrant, another one on the right, decreasing from the second to fourth quadrant.

Transformed Functions

Right Cutlass

\( g(x) = 8.2\cdot \dfrac{1}{0.1(x+6.1)^2} - 3.7,\quad -2.4 \le x \le 5 \)

  • Vertically stretch by a factor of 8.2
  • Horizontally stretch by a factor of 10
  • Horizontally translate 6.1 units to the left
  • Vertically translate 3.7 units downwards

Left Cutlass

\( j(x) = 8.2\cdot \dfrac{1}{0.1(x-6.6)^2} - 3.7,\quad -3.5 \le x \le 2.9 \)

  • Vertically stretch by a factor of 8.2
  • Horizontally stretch by a factor of 10
  • Horizontally translate 6.6 units to the right
  • Vertically translate 3.7 units downwards

Explanation

We took the right part of the parent function, as it is decreasing from the second to the fourth quadrant. To make the parent function reach the pirate cutlass below, we made a vertical translation 3.7 units downwards. But the bottom left corner is too vertical; to make it smoother, we chose to vertically stretch by a factor of 8.2. Now, the bottom left corner has become much flatter. However, compared to the curve of the pirate cutlass, the upper part is still too vertical, so we decided to horizontally stretch it by a factor of 10.

Another pirate cutlass is modeled similarly using the other branch of the parent function, with horizontal translation adjusted to the right. In both cases, the transformations allow the curves to closely match the decorative blades.

Domain and Range

  • \(g(x)\) Domain: \([-2.4, 5]\), Range: \([-3, 2.3]\) (rounded)
  • \(j(x)\) Domain: \([-3.5, 2.9]\), Range: \([-2.9, 2.3]\) (rounded)

3. Polynomial Function – Cubic Roller Coaster

At Sunway Lagoon, this roller coaster is the most thrilling and exciting. Moreover, the track on this section of the roller coaster perfectly coincides with a cubic function. We chose a cubic function to fit this image, as this roller coaster track has a highest and lowest point, which matches the characteristics of the cubic function.

Parent Function

\( y = x(x-5.6)(x+8.6) \)

Because the entire ride could not be fully photographed from the photographer’s angle, we estimated the key points in order to determine the function. This function passes through the point \((0, 0)\). We also speculated that it has x-intercepts at approximately \((5.6, 0)\) and \((-8.6, 0)\), which is why we selected this curve as our parent function.

We spotted this track while waiting in line for the roller coaster, which reminded us of the polynomial function. The track had a highest and lowest point, perfectly matching our expectations for the polynomial function, so we chose it.

Transformed Function

\( y = 0.0121 \cdot x(x-5.6)(x+8.6) + 0.1,\quad -5 \le x \le 4.8 \)

  • Vertically compress by a factor of 0.0121
  • Vertically translate up 0.1 units

Explanation

As mentioned in the previous text, we speculated this function passes through the points \((0,0)\), \((5.6,0)\) and \((-8.6,0)\), so we had our parent function. However, the portion between \((-8.6,0)\) and \((0,0)\) is too steep as well as the part between \((0,0)\) and \((5.6,0)\); as a result, we vertically compressed the function by a factor of 0.0121. Then we found the function is too low compared to the image, so we vertically translate the function 0.1 unit up.

Domain and Range

Because of the limitation of the image, we had to restrict the domain and range of this function. The function decreases from the point \((-5, 2.4)\) to \((3.1, -1)\) (rounded), passing through the point \((0, 0)\). Then, the function starts to increase from \((3.1, -1)\) to \((4.8, -0.52)\) (rounded). So we have:

  • Domain: \([-5, 4.8]\)
  • Range: \([-1, 2.4]\) (rounded)

4. Exponential Function – Gentle Water Slide

Next, let’s move to Sunway Lagoon’s water park. The first ride is a water slide, representing the exponential function. It is probably the most relaxing and gentle ride in the entire park.

Parent Function

\( f(x) = 0.5^x \)

Below is an image of the ideal ride that best matches the parent function, which helps visualize why we chose this ride.

This slide looks very similar to the lower part of a horizontally stretched exponential function, and the ground acts as its asymptote.

Transformed Functions

First Function

\( f(x) = 4.1\left(\dfrac12\right)^{0.19(x+16)} - 3.6,\quad 5 \le x \le 10 \)

  • Vertical stretch by a factor of 4.1
  • Horizontal stretch by a factor of 5.26 (≈ \(1/0.19\))
  • Horizontal translation 16 units to the left
  • Vertical translation 3.6 units downwards

Second Function

\( g(x) = 4.1\left(\dfrac12\right)^{0.19(x+8.8)} - 4,\quad -10 \le x \le 5 \)

  • Vertical stretch by a factor of 4.1
  • Horizontal stretch by a factor of 5.26
  • Horizontal translation 8.8 units to the left
  • Vertical translation 4 units downwards

Explanation

This slide goes from left to right, which means it shows a decreasing trend. We chose a base between 0 and 1 for our exponential function. However, because the graph of the function increases too rapidly as it approaches negative infinity, while the slope of the slide is much gentler, we added an additional function to fully match the ride.

We first moved the function downward by 3.5 units to bring its asymptote closer to the ground. Then, because it changed too rapidly when approaching negative infinity, we shifted it 8 units to the left, at which point the graph intersected the slide. The left half of the graph had a very large curvature, so we applied a horizontal stretch by a factor of 5 (≈ \(1/0.2\)) to make it flatter.

Since the graph was below the slide, we applied a vertical stretch by a factor of 4. At this point, the graph was almost aligned with the ride, but it was still slightly above it, so we moved it downward by another 0.5 units (a total translation of 4 units). We then made some slight adjustment by changing the horizontal stretch from 5 to 5.26 and the vertical stretch from 4 to 4.1. At this point, the graph aligned perfectly with the ride except for a small portion on the far right. Because this section of the slide is almost a straight line, we modified the position of the asymptote of the same function to the upper left to create a new function. Finally, the two functions together covered the entire ride.

Domain and Range

Because the slide is very gentle, the ranges of the two functions do not vary significantly. The range of the left function is between –3.33 and 0.8. Since the part on the right lies very close to the ground and is almost a straight line, its range is even smaller, from –3.46 to –3.34. The domains of the two functions connect with no gap, covering the entire slide from –10 to 10.

5. Logarithmic Function – Freefall Tube Slide

Next is the logarithmic function, modeled after the Freefall Tube Slide from Sunway Lagoon. In contrast to the last ride, this one is an extreme drop slide with a slope that is almost 90 degrees. The two rides form a strong contrast, just like exponential and logarithmic functions, which are inverse to each other.

Parent Function

\( f(x) = \log_{0.5}(x) \)

Below is an image of the ideal ride that best matches the parent function, which helps visualize why we chose this ride.

Transformed Function

\( g(x) = 8\log_{0.5}\big(2.8(x+1)\big) + 30,\quad 1.3 \le x \le 6.4 \)

  • Vertical stretch by a factor of 8
  • Horizontal compression by a factor of 0.357 (≈ \(1/2.8\))
  • Horizontal translation 1 unit to the left
  • Vertical translation 30 units upwards

Explanation

Unlike the last function, this ride is much steeper than the parent logarithmic function. So we first applied a vertical stretch by a factor of 4 to make the curve match the shape of the slide. After applying the vertical and horizontal translations, we noticed a similar issue as before: only the middle part aligned with the ride because the curvature did not match the slide.

To increase the steepness, we strengthened the vertical stretch to a factor of 8. Even then, the angle was still not steep enough, so we tried a horizontal compression by a factor of 1/3. After observing the graph, we found that the part where the curvature increases rapidly closely matched the downward slope of the slide. We then moved the curved part upward by 30 units. At this point, the graph was very close, but it was slightly too far to the right, so we shifted it 1 unit to the left.

Then, we realized that the horizontal compression of 1/3 was a bit too strong, so we adjusted it to 1/2.8 (changing from approximately 0.33 to about 0.357). Finally, the graph fits almost the entire slide perfectly.

Domain and Range

This time, because the Freefall Tube Slide is extremely steep, its domain is relatively small, ranging from 1.3 to 6.4. However, its range is much larger, from approximately –5 to 8.5.

  • Domain: \([1.3, 6.4]\)
  • Range: \([-5, 8.5]\) (rounded)

6. Cosecant Function – Tall Launch Coaster

Six Flags Great Adventure, New Jersey, USA

This is a hydraulically launched steel roller coaster at Six Flags Great Adventure in New Jersey, United States. It was known as the tallest and fastest roller coaster in the world when it opened in 2005.

Parent Function

\( f(x) = \csc x \)

Below is an image of the ideal ride that best matches the parent function, which helps visualize why we chose this ride.

Transformed Function

\( f(x) = 1.9\,\csc\big(0.52(x-2.9)\big) + 11,\quad -2.96 \le x \le 2.72 \)

  • Vertical stretch by a factor of 1.9
  • Horizontal stretch by a factor of \(25/13\)
  • Horizontal translation 2.9 units to the right
  • Vertical translation 11 units upwards

Explanation

We observed that the bottom part, which is below the x-axis, has a curve at the top. In contrast, the upper part of the function has a curve at the bottom. In this case, we chose to use the bottom part which is below the x-axis. First, we raised it up because it was under the x-axis. Then, we noticed that the curve at the top was too narrow, so we applied a horizontal stretch by a factor of \(25/13\). However, the function still deviated from the image, so we slightly moved the function 2.9 units to the right. Finally, the curves at the top left and top right corners were too blunt, and the function was a bit too high, so we made an improvement: vertically stretch by a factor of 1.9.

Domain and Range

As the length of this roller coaster was limited by the “photographer”, we have to restrict its domain and range. The domain of this part starts from –2.96 to 2.72. The range of this portion is quite interesting: it starts growing from –9.2, then reaches its peak at 9.1, finally decreases to –9.3. The lowest point is –9.3, so we use:

  • Domain: \([-2.96, 2.72]\)
  • Range: \([-9.3, 9.1]\) (rounded)

7. Sinusoidal Function – The Joker

Six Flags Over Texas, Arlington, USA

The second trigonometric function is a sine function, modeled after The Joker from Six Flags Over Texas in Arlington, United States, one of the silliest-looking rollercoasters you may ever see, but the screams you’ll hear coming from this ride are no joke!

The upper contour shows a clear repeating crest–trough pattern that can be well modeled by a sinusoidal function. Here we use sine as an example.

Parent Function

\( f(x) = \sin x \)

Transformed Functions

\( g(x) = \sin\big(0.75(x-1.5)\big) + 3.6,\quad -8 \le x \le -3 \)

  • Horizontal stretch by a factor of \(4/3\)
  • Horizontal translation 1.5 units to the right
  • Vertical translation 3.6 units upwards
  • Domain: \([-8, -3]\), Range: \([2.85, 4.6]\) (rounded)

\( g(x) = \sin\big(0.6(x-2.15)\big) + 3.9,\quad -3 \le x \le 2 \)

  • Horizontal stretch by a factor of \(5/3\)
  • Horizontal translation 2.15 units to the right
  • Vertical translation 3.9 units upwards
  • Domain: \([-3, 2]\), Range: \([2.90, 3.85]\) (rounded)

\( g(x) = \sin\big(0.8(x-1.85)\big) + 3.67,\quad 2 \le x \le 5 \)

  • Horizontal stretch by a factor of 1.25 (\(1/0.8\))
  • Horizontal translation 1.85 units to the right
  • Vertical translation 3.67 units upwards
  • Domain: \([2, 5]\), Range: \([3.79, 4.67]\) (rounded)

\( g(x) = 0.95\sin\big(0.6(x-0.2)\big) + 4,\quad 5 \le x \le 10 \)

  • Vertical compression by a factor of 0.95
  • Horizontal stretch by a factor of \(5/3\)
  • Horizontal translation 0.2 units to the right
  • Vertical translation 4 units upwards
  • Domain: \([5, 10]\), Range: \([3.05, 4.25]\)

Explanation

Interestingly, the four functions use almost the same transformations: the amplitude \(A\) remains nearly unchanged (approximately 1). As the periods of these functions are slightly longer than the parent function, we stretched them horizontally by factors of roughly 0.78 on average to fit the image. The phase shifts \(d\) and vertical translations \(c\) vary only to adjust local alignment.

The four domain intervals are contiguous and meet exactly at the endpoints: \([-8,-3]\), \([-3,2]\), \([2,5]\), \([5,10]\), together covering \([-8,10]\) with no gaps. Because the amplitude is almost the same across the functions, the range is nearly identical as well, lying approximately between 2.93 and 4.7 (rounded).

8. Secant Function – U-Shape Skateboard Ride

Chimelong Paradise, Guangzhou, China

The last trigonometric function is the secant function, modeled after the U-shape skateboard ride from Chimelong Paradise in Guangzhou, China. The inside of this ride forms a big bowl that matches the upper part of the secant function.

Parent Function

\( m(x) = \sec x \)

Below is an image of the ideal ride that best matches the parent function, which helps visualize why we chose this ride.

Transformed Function

\( m(x) = 4\sec\big(0.15(x+0.2)\big) - 7.38,\quad -7.7 \le x \le 7.3 \)

  • Vertical stretch by a factor of 4
  • Horizontal stretch by a factor of \(20/3\)
  • Horizontal translation 0.2 units to the left
  • Vertical translation 7.38 units downwards

Explanation

The first step that we took is stretching horizontally to align the bottom of the function graph with the opening of the amusement park ride. However, the bottom of the function is still flat, so we stretched the function vertically by a factor of 4. After this, we got a good curve on the graph, but we needed to drag it down since it was still above the ride. We translated it down 7.38 units. Finally, to make the curve fit the image even better, we translated it 0.2 units to the left.

Domain and Range

Although this graph shows the whole ride, we still need to limit its domain and range. The function starts decreasing from \((-7.7, 1.9)\) (rounded), reaches its lowest point at \((0,-3.38)\) (rounded), and then increases to \((7.3,1.9)\) (rounded). So we have:

  • Domain: \([-7.7, 7.3]\)
  • Range: \([-3.4, 1.9]\) (rounded)

9. Absolute Value Functions – La Grande Roue de Montréal

This is La Grande Roue de Montréal, an iconic attraction open 365 days a year. Canada's tallest observation wheel, standing at 60 meters tall, offers 18-minute rotations, providing you with breathtaking 360-degree views of Montreal.

We chose the Ferris wheel as our model because its spokes are very straight, extending from the center outwards, making it the perfect choice for an absolute value function model.

Parent Function

\( y = |x| \)

Transformed Functions

\( y = 3|x|,\quad -0.9 \le x \le 0.9 \)

  • Vertical stretch by a factor of 3
  • Domain: \([-0.9, 0.9]\), Range: \([0, 2.7]\)

\( y = 1.35|x|,\quad -1.8 \le x \le 1.8 \)

  • Vertical stretch by a factor of 1.35
  • Domain: \([-1.8, 1.8]\), Range: \([0, 2.43]\)

\( y = 0.72|x|,\quad -2.5 \le x \le 2.5 \)

  • Vertical compression by a factor of 0.72
  • Domain: \([-2.5, 2.5]\), Range: \([0, 1.8]\)

\( y = 0.355|x|,\quad -3 \le x \le 3 \)

  • Vertical compression by a factor of 0.355
  • Domain: \([-3, 3]\), Range: \([0, 1.065]\)

\( y = 0.07|x|,\quad -3.2 \le x \le 3.2 \)

  • Vertical compression by a factor of 0.07
  • Domain: \([-3.2, 3.2]\), Range: \([0, 0.224]\)

\( y = -0.223|x|,\quad -3.2 \le x \le 3.2 \)

  • Reflection on the x-axis
  • Vertical compression by a factor of 0.223
  • Domain: \([-3.2, 3.2]\), Range: \([-0.7136, 0]\)

\( y = -0.55|x|,\quad -2.8 \le x \le 2.8 \)

  • Reflection on the x-axis
  • Vertical compression by a factor of 0.55
  • Domain: \([-2.8, 2.8]\), Range: \([-1.54, 0]\)

\( y = -1.1|x|,\quad -2.15 \le x \le 2.15 \)

  • Reflection on the x-axis
  • Vertical stretch by a factor of 1.1
  • Domain: \([-2.15, 2.15]\), Range: \([-2.365, 0]\)

\( y = -2|x|,\quad -1.5 \le x \le 1.5 \)

  • Reflection on the x-axis
  • Vertical stretch by a factor of 2
  • Domain: \([-1.5, 1.5]\), Range: \([-3, 0]\)

\( y = -6.5|x|,\quad -0.52 \le x \le 0.52 \)

  • Reflection on the x-axis
  • Vertical stretch by a factor of 6.5
  • Domain: \([-0.52, 0.52]\), Range: \([-3.38, 0]\)

Explanation

Absolute value functions are quite straightforward to explain. If they lie above the x-axis, then they don't need to be reflected about the x-axis. Conversely, if they lie below the x-axis, then they do, and we add a negative sign in front of the function. All we need to do is vertically stretch, compress, or reflect it on the x-axis.

We are describing the function in terms of the spokes of a Ferris wheel, so we need to restrict the range and domain from the central axle to the cabins. We consider the central axis of the Ferris wheel as the origin of the function and the cabin as the endpoint to restrict the range and domain, which gives us the domain and range for each function.
